What your competitors are already doing

SNHU, Capella, Purdue Global, and UMGC are competing for the same adult learner. Here is where each one has already solved a problem UAGC hasn't.

Based on direct review of live homepages and program pages, April 2026.

Southern New Hampshire University
snhu.edu  ·  189,000+ enrolled students
Strongest overall
✓
Above the fold: "Get your degree, your way" with "Find a Program" as sole dominant hero CTA. "Request Info" is secondary and clearly subordinate. No competing elements in the hero viewport.
✓
Cost transparency: States $342/credit for undergrad explicitly on the homepage. Runs a direct cost comparison naming competitors — including UAGC at $499/credit. Adult learners can assess ROI without clicking anywhere.
✓
UAGC in copy: "SNHU" appears once in the header. Hero copy is entirely student-centered: "your future," "your terms," "your success." Institution name does not appear in body copy.
✓

✕
White space: Mid-page sections are dense with content, icons, and testimonials stacked closely — the one area where UAGC could differentiate on clarity.
UAGC opportunity
SNHU wins on above-fold clarity and copy discipline. The gap UAGC can exploit: SNHU's pages are dense mid-scroll. A cleaner, more modular program page experience with Lightcast data prominently placed is a differentiator SNHU doesn't currently offer.
Purdue Global
purdueglobal.edu  ·  "Purdue's online university for working adults"
Strong on ROI framing
✓
Career-first copy: Homepage headline is "Don't just go back to school — come back and move forward." Every headline speaks to the returning adult's career situation, not the institution's credentials.
✓
Cost + time calculator: "On average, bachelor's degree students pay $15k and take 2.3 years" is surfaced prominently with an interactive calculator. Adult learners can model their own scenario before inquiring.

UAGC opportunity
Purdue Global has the best cost-and-time transparency in the competitive set. UAGC should match this: a visible, interactive calculator showing cost and time-to-complete based on transfer credits is a direct competitive response — and directly addresses the 40% of adult learners who cite financial anxiety as their top barrier.

Modular blocks with clear spacing — each fact has room to land before the next one
Concern 01 — White space
Dense layouts make adult learners work harder — and they won't
An adult learner researching on a lunch break or after the kids are in bed is scanning, not reading. DBU's Awwwards-recognized program page uses modular blocks with clear breathing room between each fact — credential type, duration, outcomes — so the eye knows exactly where to land. Dense, continuous content reads as noise. When everything competes for attention, nothing gets it.
What to fix — White space
Break program page content into distinct modules with visible separation: program overview → career outcomes → cost and schedule → admissions. Each module should be completable in a single glance. Increased white space between sections is not emptiness — it is the visual signal that tells a scanning adult learner where one idea ends and the next begins.
Model: DBU "in a nutshell" modular layout · Waldorf School of Garden City (Awwwards 2025) program page spacing
Lincoln College · Awwwards Nominee 2024
Entire above-the-fold viewport dedicated to one task: "Search for a course"
Concern 02 — Above the fold
The first screen should answer one question: what do I do to find the right program?
Lincoln College scored the highest above-fold clarity in the entire benchmark by making the hero do exactly one job — "Search for a course." No RFI widget, no chat prompt, no competing navigation. UAGC's current above-the-fold presents four elements simultaneously: hero CTA, RFI button, chat widget, and navigation. Adult learners returning across multiple sessions need a clear re-entry point — not a decision about which surface to engage.
What to fix — Above the fold
One primary action above the fold: "Explore programs" — linked to a program directory filtered by career goal. One secondary action at lower visual weight: "Request info." Everything else moves below the fold or into the header. The adult learner who arrives with a specific career question needs to see a path to that answer in the first three seconds — not a choice between four competing surfaces.
Model: Lincoln College (5/5 above-fold clarity) · Northwood University "Find your program" hero pattern
Middlesex University Dubai · Awwwards 2023 — warning case
"Apply Now" header + dominant "Enquire Now" form: two high-commitment CTAs competing for the same decision
Concern 03 — Multiple RFIs
The issue isn't that they all point to the same place — it's that the ask appears before the learner is ready to say yes
All of UAGC's RFIs lead to the same form, so there's no duplication of destination. The problem is duplication of the ask at the wrong moment. Each time an adult learner dismisses "Request Info" because they're still evaluating, they become less likely to respond to it when they finally are ready. After two or three dismissals, the form becomes invisible — including at the one moment it would have converted. Middlesex University Dubai shows the visual version of this: two high-commitment CTAs in the same viewport force a choice before the learner has established intent.
What to fix — Multiple RFIs
One RFI placement per page type, at the moment of highest readiness. On program pages: after the career outcomes module and before admissions requirements — when the adult learner has just seen the salary data and is ready to take the next step. On the homepage: accessible from the secondary CTA only, not repeated in the hero body. Consolidate into a single "Next step" component so every page has one clear conversion moment, not several competing ones.
Model: Lincoln College course detail — single "Apply Now" CTA, zero competition · Avoid: Middlesex Dubai dual-CTA pattern
Ashland University · Awwwards Nominee 2023
"We see you" — the adult learner addressed before the institution name appears in copy
Concern 04 — Use of UAGC in page copy
Every time "UAGC" appears in body copy, it displaces a career fact that could be there instead
Ashland University's hero says "We see you" — the institution name sits in the header where it belongs, and the copy earns the scroll. When "UAGC" is repeated throughout program page body copy, it fills space that should be occupied by the information adult learners actually need: job titles, salary ranges, skills employers want. Brand trust is built through design and outcomes evidence — not through repetition.
What to fix — UAGC in page copy
Use "UAGC" once per page — in the header or as a single trust anchor above the fold. From that point, copy should speak in second person and program-specific terms: "you'll develop," "roles you can pursue," "employers in this field look for." Program page H1 = the degree name. The next line = a career outcome, not the institution name. CTAs = action-oriented ("Request info," "Talk to an advisor") — not brand-repetitive.
Model: Ashland "We see you" hero copy · Wellington College (Webby Winner 2023) — brand narrative rewritten around the learner, not the institution
Northwood University · Awwwards Nominee 2024
Each section earns its place in the journey — content flows toward a decision, not away from one
Concerns 05 & 06 — Lightcast location · Chat location
Both Lightcast and chat are in the right spirit — but both are placed where the adult learner can't easily access them when it matters
Lightcast outcomes data placed at the bottom of a program page is a compliance checkbox — the adult learner who needed it to make a decision already left. UAGC's chat is the inverse problem: not too aggressive, but too passive and too friction-heavy. It lives in the footer of every page — requiring a full scroll to find — and asks for name, email, and phone before the conversation even starts. An adult learner with a quick question about credit transfer or financial aid is likely to abandon rather than complete a contact form just to ask it.
What to fix — Lightcast location
Move Lightcast to the third module on every program page — after program overview, before cost and admissions. Title: "Career outlook for this program." Show median salary, projected growth, top job titles, top skills. Source: "Data: Lightcast." CTA: "See full career outlook." For an adult learner calculating whether a degree justifies the investment, this is the decision driver — treat it like one, not like a footnote.
Model: DBU "in a nutshell" module position · Lightcast Widget Builder embeds directly at any page location
What to fix — Chat location & entry experience
Three changes: (1) Keep the footer chat as-is — it's correctly non-intrusive and labeled clearly alongside the phone number. (2) Add a secondary chat entry point on high-intent pages — program pages, tuition, financial aid, admissions requirements — as a contextual inline prompt triggered by scroll depth or dwell time, so learners don't have to hunt for it when a question surfaces mid-page. (3) Reduce the entry form friction: asking for name, email, and phone before the conversation starts is a barrier. Best practice is to open the chat window directly and collect contact details only if the learner wants a follow-up. The goal is to answer the question first — the contact information follows naturally from a good conversation.
Source: Nielsen Norman Group — "place chat on Contact/Help pages and don't rely on floating buttons as the only access point" · NN/g also notes that vague or high-friction entry reduces chat engagement significantly
